# MyRX Voice Cheat Sheet

Pin this above your desk. Every word that goes out under the MyRX name passes through here first.

---

## 1. The 3-Pillar Coach Voice Rule

Every piece of content — caption, post, email, ad, bio, template — must do all three:

**1. Acknowledge state.** Meet the reader where they are right now. What are they currently feeling, seeing, struggling with?

**2. Explain mechanism.** Name the biology, the system, the actual cause. Not "this works" — *why* it works.

**3. Name realistic next step.** Concrete action tonight, this week, this set. Not "level up your training."

---

### Bad example (hits none of three)

> Ready to transform your fitness journey? Stop making excuses and unlock the strongest version of yourself! Crush your goals with MyRX — the game-changer that's helping thousands level up. Don't miss out!

This shouts. It promises transformation. It names no mechanism. It gives no next step. It would embarrass a real coach.

### Rewrite (hits all three)

> You've been hitting 185 on bench for three weeks. That's not a plateau — that's your nervous system getting efficient at the weight. Strength gains at this point come from one heavy single, not five sets of five. Tonight: warm up, work up to one rep at 195, then go home. Recovery is the work.

State acknowledged (stuck at 185 for three weeks). Mechanism explained (neural efficiency, not muscular failure). Next step named (one heavy single at 195, then stop).

## 2. The 7 System-Message Rules on Social

| Rule | How it lands on social |
|---|---|
| **Channel coherence** | An Instagram caption refers to the carousel slides, not "the link below" if there is no link. A LinkedIn post reads like LinkedIn, not a tweet. |
| **No redundancy across blocks** | Headline ≠ first line ≠ caption body. If the hook says "stuck at the same weight for a month," the body advances — it doesn't restate. |
| **The coach leads, doesn't pay** | Never frame coaches as subsidizing athletes. The coach is the leader inviting the athlete to train. Billing is invisible mechanism. |
| **No marketing or performative phrasing** | "Start your journey" is banned. Plain factual coach voice. |
| **No security platitudes** | When mentioning data or privacy, name the actual mechanism. "Your data stays on your roster" beats "we take privacy seriously." |
| **Direct address — "you"** | Talk to one athlete at a time. Not "athletes everywhere are…" Not "our community." |
| **No filler hedges** | "You might want to consider" → "Do this." "Please try again" → "Try again." |

## 4. Banned Marketing Phrases

### Transformation family
"Transform your life," "fitness journey," "unlock," "discover the secret," "the secret to," "old you vs new you," "become the best version of yourself," "level up," "level up your training," "game changer," "biohack," "hack," "growth hack," "shortcut."

These promise a destination and skip the work. MyRX doesn't sell destinations — we name the next step.

### Performance worship family
"Crushed it," "killed it," "shredded," "ripped," "dominated," "destroyed," "unstoppable," "crush your goals," "push past your limits," "be unstoppable."

These confuse intensity with progress. The reader doesn't need to feel hyped — they need to know what to load tonight.

### Hedge family
"For your security," "we take privacy seriously," "consider," "you might want to," "feel free to," "if you'd like," "down the line," "in the future," "double-check," "Please" before an imperative.

These hedge what should be direct. A coach says "do this." Not "you might want to consider doing this."

### Urgency theater family
"Don't miss out," "Last chance," "Time is running out," "Only X spots left," "Limited time offer," "Act now."

Real coaching doesn't manufacture scarcity. The work is here whenever the athlete is ready for it.

## 5. Preferred Coach-Voice Phrasings

### How to open

- "You've felt this — the lift that used to move now sits there for a second before it goes."
- "You've seen this on your log — three weeks of the same weight, same reps, same RPE."
- "You logged a 4:50/km last Tuesday. Your endurance pace should sit at 5:50/km this week."
- "Your bench has been at 185 since February."

Open with what the reader is currently experiencing or what their log shows.

### How to explain

- "What's happening here is your nervous system getting efficient at the weight."
- "The mechanism is mitochondrial density — Z2 builds the capillaries that determine everything above."
- "This is why the eff curve predicts the move, not your willpower."
- "Hypertrophy lives at 6-12 reps because that rep range maximizes time under tension at heavy enough load to recruit the big fibers."

Name the actual biological or system mechanism. Not "this works" — *why*.

### How to close

- "Tonight: warm up, work up to one heavy single at 195, then go home."
- "Here's where to start: 30g of protein at breakfast for the next four days."
- "Next session: same weight, one more rep on the last set."
- "This week: get three lifts and one Z2 cardio in. That's the assignment."

Close with a concrete, measurable action the reader can take by the end of the week.

## 6. Social Formatting

- **Lowercase** coach, athlete, client in body copy. Capitalize only when acting as UI chrome (a section header, a label).
- **No exclamation points** on platform copy. Real coaches don't shout in writing.
- **No emojis** unless the platform expects them inline (an Instagram caption can use one; a LinkedIn post cannot).
- **Em-dashes** for thought breaks. The rhythm matters — it's how a coach speaks.
- **Brand mid-sentence, verb-first** for titles. "Sign in to MyRX" beats "MyRX — Sign In." "Build your next step with MyRX" beats "MyRX: The Next-Step Coaching Platform."

---

## 7. The 5-Question Pre-Publish Checklist

Before anything goes live, run it through these five:

1. **Does this acknowledge what the reader is currently experiencing?** If it opens with aspiration instead of state, rewrite the opener.

2. **Does it name the mechanism, not just the claim?** "This works" isn't a mechanism. "Your nervous system is getting efficient at the weight" is.

3. **Does it end with a concrete next step, not a vague rallying cry?** "Crush it" is not a next step. "Work up to one rep at 195" is.

4. **Would any sentence trigger the cringe of marketing speak?** If a real coach would never say it standing next to you on the gym floor, cut it.

5. **Does the brand sit mid-sentence + verb-first in any title or CTA?** Lead with the action. Brand sits inside the action.

If any answer is no, the piece isn't ready.
