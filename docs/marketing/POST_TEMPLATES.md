## Pillar A — Training Science

### A1. "Why does this hurt?" Mechanism Explainer

**Purpose.** Replace fitness mythology with the actual biology behind a symptom athletes feel.
**When to use.** Instagram Reel or TikTok. Anytime an athlete asks "is this normal?" — DOMS, side stitch, lactic burn, sleep crash after long runs.
**Format.** 45-second Reel. Hook (3s) → mechanism (25s) → next step (15s). One coach on camera, B-roll of the movement.

**Example (filled):**

> Hook: DOMS is not a sign you trained hard enough.
>
> Mechanism: DOMS — delayed onset muscle soreness — peaks 24 to 72 hours after eccentric loading. It's micro-tearing in the sarcomeres plus the inflammatory response that follows. It tells you the eccentric load was new or higher than your tissue had adapted to. It does NOT tell you the session was productive. Trained lifters running a 3x10 back squat at 75 percent often feel almost nothing the next day and still hit a PR the following week.
>
> Next step: stop using soreness as a workout grade. Look at your bar speed on the last set, look at whether you cleared your prescribed reps at RPE 7 or lower, look at whether your next session lands a higher number on the same lift. Those are signals. Soreness is noise.

**Voice notes.** Hits all 3 pillars. Avoids "crushed it," "shredded," and the urgency theater common to fitness content. Lowercase "athlete" throughout.
**Posting time.** Tuesday 6:15 PM ET (athletes scrolling post-session).
**Hashtags.** 4-6 — #strengthcoach #trainingscience #DOMS #recovery #liftingcoach #strengthtraining

### A2. "The Number Behind the Number" Carousel

**Purpose.** Show the math or physiology that drives a prescription. Earns authority without lecturing.
**When to use.** Instagram or LinkedIn carousel. Best for explaining anything with a formula — 1RM projection, zone 2 pace, macro split, hypertrophy rep range.
**Format.** 6-8 slide carousel. Slide 1 = hook number. Slides 2-6 = build. Final slide = next step.

**Example (filled):**

> Slide 1: "1 to 5 reps. 6 to 12 reps. 13 plus reps. Three rep ranges, three adaptations, one curve."
>
> Slide 2: Strength adaptation lives at 1 to 5 reps. Heavy loads at low reps recruit your biggest motor units and train them to fire harder and faster. The adaptation is neural — you get stronger without adding muscle size.
>
> Slide 3: Hypertrophy adaptation lives at 6 to 12 reps. Moderate loads at moderate reps maximize mechanical tension across enough total work to drive muscle protein synthesis. This is where size grows.
>
> Slide 4: Endurance adaptation lives at 13 plus reps. Lighter loads at high reps train your slow-twitch fibers and improve their capacity to clear metabolites. This is where work capacity grows.
>
> Slide 5: The same curve — the Epley curve — projects your one rep max from any of these zones. A 5 rep max at 225 projects to roughly 253. A 10 rep max at 185 projects to roughly 247. Same athlete, different zones, same underlying strength.
>
> Slide 6: Next step. Pick the adaptation your training is short on right now. If you only train at 5s, your work capacity is leaking. If you only train at 12s, your top-end strength is leaving size gains on the table. Rotate the zones across an 8 week block.

**Voice notes.** All 3 pillars. Mechanism is named (motor units, MPS, metabolite clearance). No "level up" language.
**Posting time.** Wednesday 12:30 PM ET (lunch break carousel scroll).
**Hashtags.** 5 — #strengthtraining #hypertrophy #powerlifting #liftingcoach #onerepmax

>
> Mechanism: Athlete on the left weighs 165. Three times bodyweight. That's an advanced pull on the relative-strength scale. Athlete on the right weighs 245. One-point-three times bodyweight. ### A4. "What Lactate Actually Does" Thread

**Purpose.** Long-form mechanism post for X / Threads / LinkedIn. Reframe a misunderstood concept.
**When to use.** Tuesday or Thursday on text-first platforms. Pair with a Reel that links to the thread.
**Format.** 7-10 post thread. One concept per post. End on the prescription.

**Example (filled):**

> 1/8 Lactate is not what makes your legs burn. The burn is hydrogen ions accumulating faster than your buffer system can clear them. Lactate is what your body makes to keep you going.
>
> 2/8 When your muscles run glycolysis hard, pyruvate stacks up. Pyruvate gets converted to lactate so the reaction can keep producing ATP. Lactate is fuel. Your heart and slow-twitch fibers actually prefer to burn it.
>
> 3/8 What changes between an untrained athlete and a trained one is lactate clearance — how fast you shuttle lactate back into the bloodstream and oxidize it. Trained athletes clear lactate at much higher work rates.
>
> 4/8 This is why threshold work matters. Threshold is the highest pace you can hold where your clearance roughly matches your production. Train near it and the threshold moves up.
>
> 5/8 If you only train easy, your clearance ceiling stays low. If you only train hard, you accumulate fatigue without specifically targeting clearance.
>
> 6/8 Daniels prescribes cruise intervals at exactly threshold pace — 4 to 6 reps of 1 km with 60 seconds of jog recovery. That's the canonical session.
>
> 7/8 You don't need a lactate meter. Your threshold pace is roughly your 10K race pace, or about 10 seconds per km slower than your one mile pace.
>
> 8/8 Next step. Add one threshold session per week, separated from your VO2 work by at least 48 hours. Run the 4x1km set. Track your average pace across the set. When it improves week to week, your clearance is improving.

**Voice notes.** All 3 pillars across the thread. Mechanism is named precisely. Names a coach (Daniels) — that's source attribution, not a banned phrase. No exclamation points.
**Posting time.** Tuesday 8:00 AM ET.
**Hashtags.** 3 — #runningcoach #vo2max #endurancetraining

### A5. "Common Wrong Answer" Format

**Purpose.** Address a specific bad-faith fitness claim circulating on social.
**When to use.** Whenever a trend is misleading athletes. Reels or Threads.
**Format.** Hook with the wrong claim → name why it's wrong → name what's actually true → next step.

**Example (filled):**

> Hook: You'll see content that says cardio kills your gains. It's wrong.
>
> Mechanism: The concurrent training interference effect is real but small. The data — Wilson et al. 2012 meta-analysis, Murach and Bagley 2016 — shows interference is mostly seen when athletes do 6+ days of long endurance work alongside heavy lifting. Two to three zone 2 cardio sessions per week, kept separate from your lift days by at least 6 hours, have essentially zero impact on hypertrophy. Aerobic base actually improves your between-set recovery and lets you train more total volume.
>
> Next step: add two 30-minute zone 2 sessions per week on your non-lift days. Aim for nasal-breathing pace. Track your one rep max every 4 weeks. If it's not moving, the issue is your lifting program, not your cardio.

**Voice notes.** All 3 pillars. Cites real meta-analyses. Avoids "biohack" and "the secret to."

**Purpose.** Address a common training-question misconception.

**Format.** 30s Reel.

> Hook: A coach asked — can my athlete skip the threshold zone and just train endurance plus VO2?
>
> Mechanism: This is the polarized vs pyramidal training debate. Polarized — 80 percent endurance, 20 percent VO2, almost no threshold — works for elite endurance athletes with very high training volume. >
> Next step: at typical recreational training volume, keep threshold in the rotation. One threshold session per week. The cruise interval set.

### F6. "What's The Difference Between Strength and Hypertrophy Training?" Foundation Q&A

**Purpose.** Foundational education for newer athletes.
**When to use.** Onboarding-week content. Reels.
**Format.** 30-45s Reel.

**Example (filled):**

> Hook: New athletes ask — what's actually the difference between strength training and hypertrophy training?
>
> Mechanism: Strength training is about teaching your nervous system to fire harder. Heavy loads, low reps — 1 to 5 — long rest, 3 to 5 minutes. The muscle doesn't grow much. Your ability to recruit it grows. You get stronger without getting much bigger.

> Hypertrophy training is about mechanical tension across enough work to drive muscle protein synthesis. Moderate loads, moderate reps — 6 to 12 — moderate rest, 2 to 3 minutes. The muscle grows. You add visible size.

## Quick Reference — Voice Compliance Checklist

For every template you publish, run this:

1. Does it acknowledge the athlete or coach state? (Pillar 1)
2. Does it explain a mechanism — biology, formula, system? (Pillar 2)
3. Does it name a concrete next step? (Pillar 3)
4. Did you check for banned phrases? (transform, level up, crushed it, the secret to, game changer, urgency theater, exclamation points on platform copy)
5. Is "coach" lowercase in body text? Is "athlete" lowercase?
6. Is the brand mid-sentence + verb-first in any title or CTA?
7. Are you using em-dashes for thought breaks?
8. If you mention coaching coverage, does it use the canonical form — "free as long as your coach's subscription is active" — and never frame the coach as the patron?

If all eight pass, publish.
