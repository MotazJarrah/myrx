ForewordThis is the brand book.

MyRX is a fitness coaching platform built on one principle: every person should know what their next step is, and why it matters. This document is how we keep that principle visible across every screen we ship, every email we send, every coach we onboard, every athlete we serve.

Who this is for

The team building MyRX — engineers, designers, copywriters, contributors of every kind. This is the rulebook. Everything we ship runs through it.

Coaches and partners — coaches subscribing to our platform, brand ambassadors, integration partners, and anyone presenting MyRX to clients of their own. The visual identity and voice rules below are the contract: when you put the MyRX mark on something, this is what it has to look and sound like.

Investors and stakeholders — anyone evaluating MyRX from the outside. This document is what we mean when we say "this is who we are." The technical depth here isn't padding — it's evidence that the brand is operationalized, not aspirational.

This document is a contract with the future. The visual identity, voice, and brand directives locked here are not provisional.

We don't update these to chase trends. We update them when the brand grows up — when we add a new audience, a new domain, a new market — and we update them with the same rigor we used to lock them in the first place.

Read it once end-to-end. Then keep it bookmarked. Every decision you make on behalf of MyRX should be traceable to a rule on a page here.

— The MyRX Team
Ann Arbor, Michigan

Mission

MyRX helps every person progress one step at a time across every domain that matters in their fitness.

That sentence is the spine of the company. Each phrase carries weight:

  • "Every person" — not just gym-goers, not just athletes, not just the 1% who already know what they're doing. MyRX is designed for someone at their first workout and someone at their thousandth, with the same framework adapting to where they actually are.
  • "Progress one step at a time" — we never overwhelm. The product surfaces ONE next action at a time, across every domain. The user always knows what's next, why it's the right next, and what it builds toward.
  • "Every domain that matters" — strength, cardio, mobility, body composition, nutrition, recovery, hydration, sleep, and the habits that hold it all together. A coach helps with all of those, so MyRX has to too. Breadth is not bloat — it's the table-stakes for the audience we serve.

The mission is not "help people lose weight." It's not "build the best workout tracker." It's not "be the Notion of fitness." Those are tactics, products, comparisons. MyRX is something specific: a coaching layer that meets every person where they are and points at the next step. That's what we do, and it's all we do.

Vision

One product, two audiences.

Audience A
Coaches

A complete admin platform to run their entire client roster.

Audience B
Self-Coached Athletes

A coach-quality next-step experience without a human coach.

Both audiences use the same client-facing app. The coach version is the admin overlay on top of it; the self-coached version is the client UI minus the coach. There is one product, not two.

The vision is to become the default coaching layer for human performance — the layer everyone in fitness ultimately operates on top of. When the world thinks "structured fitness coaching, in software form," they think MyRX, the same way they think Stripe for payments or Figma for design files.

Revenue Model

Two streams, both built on the same product.

Stream A — Coach subscription (B2B2C)

Coaches pay a monthly subscription for the admin portal. Their clients get the full client app at no cost as long as the coach's subscription is active. The coach gets every client's data, chat, progress dashboards, and the ability to message and program for the whole roster. This is our category-defining differentiator: no other consumer fitness app sells a coach overlay this complete.

Stream B — App Store / Google Play (B2C direct)

Free download with a limited free tier. Full features unlocked by either a recurring subscription or a one-time lifetime purchase. No coach involved — the app itself plays the coaching role through the "next step" framing built into every domain.

Both streams reinforce each other. The B2C tier proves the coaching framework works without a human in the loop. The B2B2C tier proves coaches will pay for what the product enables them to do at scale. Together they form a defensible position no single-audience competitor can attack from one side without losing the other.

Values

Five values that govern how we build and operate. Not posters — rules we make decisions against.

1. Honest by default

We never tell a user something is true when it isn't. If a calculation is a projection, we say so. If a prescription is an estimate, we say so. Trust is the moat. Once a user catches us being slick, the moat is gone.

2. Coach voice, not consumer voice

We speak like a coach who knows the user, not a marketer trying to convert them. Every word that touches a user runs through the 3-pillar coach-voice filter. No exceptions. The entire next section is devoted to this.

3. Mechanism over authority

When we explain WHY something works, we cite the underlying mechanism — glycogen depletion, motor-unit recruitment, lactate clearance, joint loading. Not "experts recommend" or "research shows." This treats the user as an adult and earns trust as a source of substance.

4. The next step, not the master plan

Long-term goals matter. But the only thing we put in front of the user is the next step they can take this week. Master plans induce paralysis; next steps induce momentum.

5. Cross-platform identical, by default

Web and mobile are one product, not two. A user on web in the morning and on mobile in the evening should see the same data, in the same shape, with the same voice. We don't allow surface drift.

Audience.
Two distinct audiences served by one product. Understanding both is essential to writing copy, designing surfaces, prioritizing features.

Audience A — Coaches

Primary · B2B2C engine

Coaches pay the subscription, bring their athletes onto the platform, and live in the admin portal every day. They are the audience whose retention matters most.

Profile

  • Strength and conditioning coaches running independent practices or working inside a gym
  • Personal trainers operating remotely or in-person with rosters of 5–50 clients
  • CrossFit / functional fitness gym owners managing their members' programming
  • Sports performance coaches working with athletes across multiple sports
  • Online coaches whose entire practice runs through software

What they need

  • A complete admin platform — roster, programming, progress tracking, messaging, billing
  • Tools that make their existing coaching better, not tools that replace their judgment
  • A client-facing app that's good enough that clients actually use it (adherence is their livelihood)
  • White-label-grade polish — the app reflects on them, so it has to look professional
  • Honest, evidence-based defaults so they don't fight the product to do their job

What they don't need

  • "AI coaching" features that pretend to replace them. Coaches don't pay to be replaced.
  • Marketing-flavored gamification that makes their professional practice feel like a consumer app
  • Generic templated content that doesn't respect their specific methodology

How we treat them

The admin portal is dense, fast, professional. No animations that delay actions. No marketing copy. Pure tool. We never communicate with their clients in our voice instead of theirs — the coach is the relationship; we are the infrastructure.

Audience B — Self-Coached Athletes

B2C direct · App Store, Play, Stripe Web

Individuals who buy the app directly without a coach. They're paying for a coach-quality experience without a human in the loop.

Profile

  • Experienced lifters and runners who know what they're doing but want structured tracking and guidance
  • Athletes between coaches (post-college, between coaching contracts)
  • People who can't afford a personal coach but won't settle for a generic workout tracker
  • Data-driven athletes who want to understand WHY their programming works, not just follow it
  • People with a real performance goal — a race, a meet, a body composition target — who need a system

What they need

  • A product that treats them like an adult who can handle real information
  • The same depth a coach would provide, surfaced through the app's framework
  • Confidence the prescriptions are evidence-based, not marketing
  • A clean, fast surface they can use mid-workout without friction

What they don't need

  • A coach replacement (they've decided they don't want one)
  • Hand-holding or motivational copy
  • Social features that turn fitness into a competition

How we treat them

The client app's coaching layer speaks directly to them in coach voice, with the same depth as if a real coach were behind it. The product never apologizes for being technical — if anything, we err toward more information, more rationale, more depth. We respect their time. No upsell modals, no nag screens, no dark patterns.

Both audiences use the same client-facing app. The coach's clients see exactly what the self-coached athlete sees. The coach gets an admin overlay — extra power, extra views, extra controls — but the underlying product is identical.

Voice & Tone.
The most important section in this book. It overrides every other rule if they conflict.

The Core Idea

MyRX is a coach in software form. A real coach does not pick options off a menu and rate them like Yelp reviews. A real coach does not treat physiological adaptations like consumer preferences. A real coach does not lecture before listening. A real coach does not assume the user could "just" do something they currently can't.

A real coach DOES acknowledge where the user actually is, today. Explains why their body responds the way it does. Names a realistic next step that fits THIS user, THIS week. Trusts the user to make their own choices once they understand.

Every screen, every chip, every empty state, every error message, every onboarding line lands somewhere on this spectrum — and we're always moving toward the coach side.

The 3-Pillar Rule

Any user-facing string that touches a choice, a limit, a state, or a goal MUST do all three of these things, in order:

Pillar 1
Acknowledge

Name the user's current state honestly. Their weight, body fat, training history, lifestyle, starting point. Acknowledgment first builds trust. Lecturing without acknowledgment is presumption.

Pillar 2
Explain Biology

Connect what their body is doing to why this matters. Cite mechanism — glycogen depletion, lean tissue cannibalization, NEAT compensation — not authority. Mechanism teaches. Authority deflects.

Pillar 3
Name a Next Step

Concrete, achievable in 1–4 weeks, accounting for where they actually are. Banned hedges: "consider," "you might want to," "eventually," "down the line."

Where the rule applies

Warning chips, empty states, locked features, goal banners, info pills, tile labels (where space allows), edit chips, onboarding copy, error messages, even "coming soon" placeholders. If a string touches a user, it runs through the filter.

Three Voice Modes

Every coach-voice surface hits all three registers simultaneously.

A. Coach voice (the base)

The 3-pillar rule. Every sentence runs through this filter.

B. Progression voice

Every advisory ends with a concrete next step achievable in 1–4 weeks, NOT a vague "consider doing X someday." Even "stay here for now and revisit when your body fat drops below 20%" is a concrete step — the user has a clear marker.

Banned: "you should eventually," "down the line," "in the future you might want to."
Preferred: "right now, here's what works," "in 4 weeks, log your weight again — if you've dropped 4 lb, we'll tighten the deficit."

C. Path voice

The user is always on a NAMED path with a NEXT named milestone. We don't ask "what do you want to do?" — we say "based on where you are, you're on the [X] path. Next stop: [Y]."

Tier systems across the product (strength's adp zones, cardio's E/T/V zones, carry's BEGINNER → STRONGMAN, Rucking's GoRuck tiers) are all path-voice surfaces. Every domain should graduate to this.

The Merged Template

When writing any new user-facing string:

"You're starting from [acknowledged state]. Here's why your body does [observation]: [biology]. The next checkpoint on your path is [milestone] — most people get there in [timeframe] by [concrete action]."

Apply this template, then trim for tone. The structure should be invisible to the reader; the rhythm should feel like a coach talking.

Voice Across Surfaces

In-app (mobile + web)Tight, declarative, no marketing flourishes. The user is mid-task; respect their attention.
EmailSlightly more expansive. Subject lines verb-first ("Confirm your email on MyRX," not "Welcome!"). Short paragraphs.
SMS / OTPStripped to facts. "Your MyRX verification code is: 123456." Nothing else.

Clear Space

Minimum clear space around any logo equals the height of the "M" letter. Nothing else — no text, no graphics, no UI chrome — may enter that clear space.

The Icon variant has built-in safe-zone padding. The visible wordmark sits inside the inner ~72% of the canvas, leaving ~14% padding on each side. This matches iOS app icon guidelines and Android's adaptive icon safe zone. Do not crop the Icon variant.

Logo Don'ts

  • Stretch, skew, or rotate any logo variant. Always preserve aspect ratio.
  • Recolor the wordmark. The four locked colors are non-negotiable. No gold, no rainbow, no seasonal versions.
  • Add effects — no drop shadows, no glows, no gradients, no outlines around letters.
  • Place on busy photos without a solid-color underlay. If background is photographic, place the logo inside a dark or light solid panel.
  • Substitute typography. The wordmark is custom; don't recreate it in another typeface.
  • Crop the Icon variant. Its padding is intentional and protected by the safe-zone rule.
  • Mix variants in a single composition. One logo per surface.
  • Render the brand name as plain text as a substitute for the wordmark. If the wordmark won't fit, redesign the layout.

One Wordmark Per Surface

If a page has a centered wordmark in the body, the header must NOT also show a wordmark. If the header shows a wordmark, the body must NOT include another. One mark per surface. Always. This rule prevents the "branded-into-uselessness" feeling where the logo is repeated three times in one composition.

The Slogan Reservation

The Tag variant — wordmark + "Performance Lab" tagline — is reserved for hero placements only. It does not appear in the running app, in email signatures, on social profile avatars, or anywhere else where the wordmark needs to function as a quick identifier. The tagline is too long to read at small sizes, and overusing it dilutes its impact when it matters.

Why these specific values

  • Lime is yellow-green (H=73) — closer to yellow than to true green. This is what makes it electric. A true green at H=120 reads as "ecology brand"; our lime reads as "performance."
  • Dark is blue-tinted (H=220) at moderate saturation (28%) and very low lightness (10%). It looks like black at thumbnail size but reads as "intentional dark" against true black. The hue sits near-complementary to the lime (~147° apart on the color wheel), so the lime POPS against this dark instead of blending into the same family. Saturation is the lever that makes it read as blue vs slate-grey.
  • Surface is the same hue family as Dark with +2% lightness and slightly lower saturation (24%). This produces the elevation hierarchy users expect (cards sit visibly above the background) without introducing a competing hue.
  • Foreground is warm off-white (H=60 at very high lightness). Never pure white. Pure white is harsh on OLED screens and clashes against the cool dark backgrounds. Warm text on cool surface is the canonical high-contrast pattern.

Motion Principles

Motion is restrained, purposeful, and never decorative. Every animation answers a question: "What is the user's eye doing right now, and what does it need to track?"

The Seven Patterns

  1. Staggered Entrance Cascade — Cards slide in 0 / 250 / 500 ms. Page assembles in front of the user.
  2. Slot-Machine Numeric Ticker — Digits roll on value change. Used for progressive achievement, not for static labels.
  3. Pulsing Chevron — 1.5s cycle, telegraphs "swipe to navigate."
  4. Consolidated-Page Swipe — Whole-page slide with pill physically following finger. 220 px slide-off, 250 ms.
  5. Inline Expansion Panel — FadeInUp 200 ms / FadeOutUp 180 ms. Sibling LinearTransition prevents jumpy reflow.
  6. PhantomWheel Inertia + Cross-Fade — iOS-style numeric picker. 250 px/s release threshold, 0.993 deceleration.
  7. Save Button Feedback — "✓ Saved" hold for 1500 ms in the domain accent color.

Universal rules: Cubic-bezier (0.16, 1, 0.3, 1) is the standard entrance easing. 500 ms is the standard entrance duration. Nothing longer than 500 ms for interactive feedback. Never animate purely for decoration.

Email

Social Media

Social profile assets use the locked brand palette. Profile picture is always Icon White. Cover banners use the wordmark with the tagline (Tag White) on a MyRX Dark background, with generous clear space. Post content follows the coach voice rules — no marketing-speak, no fitness clichés, no gamified language.

Print

For business cards, signage, print collateral, and merchandise: highest-resolution PNG or SVG, match Pantone or CMYK approximations as closely as the print process allows, maintain clear-space rules.

Imagery

Style

  • Real athletes, not models. People who actually train, in actual training environments.
  • Honest lighting. Natural light, gym light, ambient. No glossy magazine retouching.
  • Mid-action over posed. The person is in the middle of a lift, run, stretch — not posing.
  • Variety of body types, ages, ethnicities. Fitness is universal; our imagery reflects that.
  • Equipment shown realistically. A barbell looks like a barbell, not a beauty shot.

What we avoid

  • Stock photos of "fit people in a gym." Read as fake immediately.
  • Before/after composites. Commodify body change in a way that contradicts coach voice.
  • Heavy color grading or filter overlays. Brand palette comes from chrome around the photo, not from the photo.
  • Sexualized imagery. Product is about performance, not appearance.
  • Models holding the product to camera. No "person smiling at phone" stock shots.

Section VII
Guardrails.
What we never do, in any context, without an explicit override. The lines that keep the brand from drifting.

Voice Guardrails

  • Never use consumer-choice voice. "Pick X for Y" is banned. Use coach voice.
  • Never use authority instead of mechanism. "Experts recommend" and "research shows" are not allowed.
  • Never use vague future hedges. "Consider," "you might want to," "eventually," "down the line" — banned.
  • Never use marketing clichés. "Transform your life," "fitness journey," "level up," "game changer," "unlock," "discover the secret," "before/after that will shock you" — all banned.
  • Never gamify reality. No streaks that punish missed days. No badges for compliance. No leaderboards that compare athletes against each other.
  • Never speak about the user's body in shame language. "Your problem areas," "trouble spots" — banned. Bodies are biological, not deficient.

Visual Guardrails

  • Never use pure black (#000000) on dark surfaces. Always MyRX Dark (#121721).
  • Never use pure white (#FFFFFF) on dark surfaces. Always MyRX Foreground (#F4F3EF).
  • Never substitute brand colors with off-brand equivalents. No alternate lime shades, no different greens.
  • Never use the lime as decorative chrome. Lime is for primary CTAs and brand highlights only.
  • Never mix typefaces. Geist and Geist Mono only.
  • Never use emojis as icon substitutes. Lucide icons only.
  • Never stretch, recolor, or modify the logo. The eight locked variants are the only acceptable forms.
  • Never crop the Icon variant. Its safe-zone padding is intentional.

Product Guardrails

  • Never auto-create accounts on behalf of a user. Always require explicit signup.
  • Never auto-enter financial information. Bank, credit card, account numbers must be entered by the user.
  • Never delete a user's data without explicit confirmation. Deletions require multi-step opt-in.
  • Never share user data with third parties without explicit consent. Privacy is a brand value, not a legal afterthought.
  • Never claim coaching efficacy we can't substantiate. If a feature is in beta, say so. If a projection is an estimate, label it.
  • Never use dark patterns. No nag screens, no manipulative upsells, no hidden cancel buttons.

Cross-Platform Guardrails

  • Never let web and mobile drift. When a decision lands, it lands on both surfaces simultaneously.
  • Never let admin surfaces diverge from end-user surfaces in look and feel. Admin is denser, but uses the same colors, typography, components.
  • Never ship a brand-mark variant not listed in the logo system. If a new variant is needed, add it to the brand book first.

Coaches, partners, and ambassadors requesting brand assets should email team@myrxfit.com with the specific use case. We respond within two business days with the appropriate files and usage guidelines.

Brand Misuse

If you see MyRX branding being used incorrectly — by a competitor, a partner who has gone off-brand, a coach building their own marketing — report it to team@myrxfit.com. We take brand integrity seriously and will work with the parties involved to bring usage into compliance. We treat first-time mistakes as opportunities to educate; repeated or willful misuse may result in revocation of permission to use the brand.

Brand Updates

This document is versioned. Major updates (new audience, new domain, new visual identity) are released as a new version number (1.0 → 2.0). Minor updates (clarifications, additional examples, typo fixes) bump the patch (1.0 → 1.1).

When the brand book changes:

  1. The new version is committed to the repository
  2. CLAUDE.md is updated to point at the new version's path
  3. All active surfaces are audited for compliance with the changes
  4. An announcement goes out to coaches and partners with a summary

The brand book is a contract with the future. We don't update it lightly.

Authority

The voice and tone rules in Section III, and the visual system in Section IV, are owned by the MyRX team and are non-delegable. They cannot be overridden by individual contributors, marketing partners, or external agencies without explicit team sign-off.

Color and typography tokens are locked at the code level (web/src/index.css, mobile/src/theme.ts). Changes require design review, migration plan, cross-platform sync, and CLAUDE.md update.
