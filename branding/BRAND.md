# Section I — Foundations

## Mission

**MyRX helps every person progress one step at a time across every domain that matters in their fitness.**

That sentence is the spine of the company. Each phrase carries weight:

- **"Every person"** — not just gym-goers, not just athletes, not just the 1% who already know what they're doing. MyRX is designed for someone at their first workout and someone at their thousandth, with the same framework adapting to where they actually are.
- **"Progress one step at a time"** — we never overwhelm. The product surfaces ONE next action at a time, across every domain. The user always knows what's next, why it's the right next, and what it builds toward.
- **"Every domain that matters"** — strength, cardio, mobility, body composition, nutrition, recovery, hydration, sleep, and the habits that hold it all together. A coach helps with all of those, so MyRX has to too. Breadth is not bloat — it's the table-stakes for the audience we serve.

The mission is not "help people lose weight." It's not "build the best workout tracker." It's not "be the Notion of fitness." Those are tactics, products, comparisons. MyRX is something specific: a coaching layer that meets every person where they are and points at the next step. That's what we do, and it's all we do.

## Vision

**One product, two audiences:**

1. **Coaches** who want a complete admin platform to run their entire client roster.
2. **Self-coached individuals** who want a coach-quality next-step experience even without a human coach.

Both audiences use the same client-facing app. The coach version is the admin overlay on top of it; the self-coached version is the client UI minus the coach. **There is one product, not two.**

The vision is to become the default coaching layer for human performance — the layer everyone in fitness ultimately operates on top of. When the world thinks "structured fitness coaching, in software form," they think MyRX, the same way they think Stripe for payments or Figma for design files.

## Revenue Model

Two streams, both built on the same product:

**Stream A — Coach subscription (B2B2C).** Coaches pay a monthly subscription for the admin portal. Their clients get the full client app at no cost as long as the coach's subscription is active. The coach gets every client's data, chat, progress dashboards, and the ability to message and program for the whole roster. This is our category-defining differentiator: no other consumer fitness app sells a coach overlay this complete.

**Stream B — App Store / Google Play (B2C direct).** Free download with a limited free tier. Full features unlocked by a recurring subscription — monthly, or annual at 17% off (two months free). A 14-day free trial fronts the paid tiers. No coach involved — the app itself plays the coaching role through the "next step" framing built into every domain.

Both streams reinforce each other. The B2C tier proves the coaching framework works without a human in the loop. The B2B2C tier proves coaches will pay for what the product enables them to do at scale. Together they form a defensible position no single-audience competitor can attack from one side without losing the other.

## Values

Five values that govern how we build and operate. These are not posters — they are rules we make decisions against.

### 1. Honest by default

We never tell a user something is true when it isn't. If a calculation is a projection, we say so. If a prescription is an estimate, we say so. If a piece of data is from a wearable that's slightly off, we surface the uncertainty.

This shows up in copy ("Based on your logged efforts so far…"), in data displays (projections labeled distinctly from logged values), in product decisions (we never gamify the metrics that don't deserve to be gamified). Trust is the moat. Once a user catches us being slick, the moat is gone.

### 2. Coach voice, not consumer voice

We speak like a coach who knows the user, not a marketer trying to convert them. This is so fundamental we devote the entire next section to it. Every word that touches a user runs through the 3-pillar coach-voice filter. No exceptions.

### 3. Mechanism over authority

When we explain WHY something works, we cite the underlying mechanism — glycogen depletion, motor-unit recruitment, lactate clearance, joint loading. Not "experts recommend" or "research shows." This treats the user as an adult who can handle real information, and earns their trust as a source of substance.

### 4. The next step, not the master plan

Long-term goals matter. But the only thing we put in front of the user is the next step they can take this week. Master plans induce paralysis; next steps induce momentum. Even our most data-rich screens (the dashboard, the calories page, the strength detail page) collapse the universe of possible information into "here is what to do next."

### 5. Cross-platform identical, by default

Web and mobile are one product, not two. A user logging on web in the morning and on mobile in the evening should see the same data, in the same shape, with the same voice. We don't allow surface drift. When a decision is locked, it's locked everywhere.

## Brand Personality

If MyRX were a person, what would they be like?

- **Smart, not flashy.** MyRX knows the science cold and is willing to explain it, but never lectures. The smartest person in the room rarely needs to prove it.
- **Direct, not blunt.** MyRX tells the user what they need to hear without softening it into mush, but also without bulldozing them. The truth is delivered with care, not with a hammer.
- **Patient, not soft.** MyRX expects discipline over time but doesn't shame mistakes. A missed week isn't punished, it's accommodated.
- **Curious, not dogmatic.** MyRX is grounded in evidence-based exercise science but stays open to where the science evolves. We're a Performance Lab, not a Performance Pulpit.
- **Personal, not clinical.** MyRX talks to the user as a specific human with a specific body, not as a row in a database.

The closest single-word descriptor: **trusted**. The closest two-word descriptor: **clear-eyed**. The closest three-word descriptor: **shows the math**.

# Section II — Audience

## The Two Audiences

MyRX is built for two distinct audiences, served by one product. Understanding both is essential to writing copy, designing surfaces, and prioritizing features.

### Audience A — Coaches (Primary)

Coaches are the engine of MyRX's B2B2C arm. They pay the subscription, they bring their athletes onto the platform, they live in the admin portal every day. They are the audience whose retention matters most.

**Profile:**
- Strength and conditioning coaches running independent practices or working inside a gym
- Personal trainers operating remotely or in-person with a roster of 5–50 clients
- CrossFit / functional fitness gym owners managing their members' programming
- Sports performance coaches working with athletes across multiple sports
- Online coaches whose entire practice runs through software

**What they need:**
- A complete admin platform — roster management, programming, progress tracking, messaging, billing
- Tools that make their existing coaching better, not tools that replace their judgment
- A client-facing app that's good enough that clients actually use it (because adherence is the coach's livelihood)
- White-label-grade polish — the app reflects on the coach, so it has to look professional
- Honest, evidence-based defaults so they don't have to fight the product to do their job

**What they don't need:**
- "AI coaching" features that pretend to replace them. Coaches don't pay to be replaced.
- Marketing-flavored gamification that makes their professional practice feel like a consumer app
- Generic templated content that doesn't respect their specific methodology

**How we treat them:**
- The admin portal is dense, fast, professional. No animations that delay actions. No marketing copy. Pure tool.
- We never communicate with their clients in our voice instead of the coach's voice. The coach is the relationship; we are the infrastructure.
- We document everything (this brand book included) so coaches can confidently extend the product into their own marketing.

### Audience B — Self-coached athletes (B2C)

Self-coached athletes are individuals who buy the app directly without going through a coach. They're paying for a coach-quality experience without a human in the loop.

**Profile:**
- Experienced lifters and runners who know what they're doing but want structured tracking and guidance
- Athletes between coaches (post-college, between coaching contracts, etc.)
- People who can't afford a personal coach but won't settle for a generic workout tracker
- Data-driven athletes who want to understand WHY their programming works, not just follow it
- People with a real performance goal — a race, a meet, a body composition target — who need a system

**What they need:**
- A product that treats them like an adult who can handle real information
- The same depth a coach would provide, surfaced through the app's framework
- Confidence that the prescriptions are evidence-based, not marketing
- A clean, fast surface they can use mid-workout without friction

**What they don't need:**
- A coach replacement (they've decided they don't want one)
- Hand-holding or motivational copy
- Social features that turn fitness into a competition

**How we treat them:**
- The client app's "coaching layer" speaks directly to them in coach voice, with the same depth as if a real coach were behind it
- The product never apologizes for being technical — if anything, we err on the side of more information, more rationale, more depth
- We respect their time. No upsell modals, no nag screens, no dark patterns.

## What the Two Audiences Share

Both audiences use the **same client-facing app**. The coach's clients see exactly what the self-coached athlete sees. This is a critical design constraint:

- The app must be excellent without a coach, AND
- The app must augment (never replace) a real coach when there is one

The way we resolve this: the coach gets an admin OVERLAY (extra power, extra views, extra controls) on top of the client app. The self-coached athlete just doesn't see the overlay — but the underlying product is identical.

# Section III — Voice and Tone

This is the most important section of the brand book. It overrides every other rule in this document if they conflict.

## The Core Idea

MyRX is a coach in software form. A real coach does not pick options off a menu and rate them like Yelp reviews. A real coach does not treat physiological adaptations like consumer preferences. A real coach does not lecture before listening. A real coach does not assume the user could "just" do something they currently can't. A real coach does not reward perfection while punishing reality.

A real coach DOES acknowledge where the user actually is, today. Explains why their body responds the way it does. Names a realistic next step that fits THIS user, THIS week. Trusts the user to make their own choices once they understand.

Every screen, every chip, every empty state, every error message, every onboarding line MUST land somewhere on this spectrum, and we're always moving toward the coach side.

## The 3-Pillar Rule (Mandatory)

Any user-facing string that touches a choice, a limit, a state, or a goal MUST do all three of these things, in order:

### Pillar 1 — Acknowledge

Name the user's current state honestly. Their weight, body fat band, training history, lifestyle, starting point — whatever's relevant to this specific message.

Acknowledgment first builds trust. Lecturing without acknowledgment is presumption.

### Pillar 2 — Explain biology

Connect the dots between what their body is doing and why this advice matters. Cite **mechanism** — glycogen depletion, lean tissue cannibalization, NEAT compensation, motor-unit recruitment, joint loading, insulin sensitivity — not **authority** — "ACSM says," "experts recommend," "research shows."

The mechanism is what teaches the user. Authority is what we lean on when we don't want to think hard enough to explain the mechanism.

### Pillar 3 — Name a realistic next step

Concrete, achievable in 1–4 weeks, accounting for where they currently are. "Stay here for now and revisit when X changes" is a valid next step — the user has a clear marker. What's not allowed: "consider," "you might want to," "eventually," "down the line," "in the future."

Vague future-tense hedging gives the user no traction. The next step has to be something they can do this week.

## Where the 3-Pillar Rule Applies

- Warning chips on wizards (pace, macros, activity choices)
- Empty states ("You haven't logged any cardio yet")
- Locked features ("Cardio coaching is in progress — log a few sessions and your zones populate")
- Goal banners (reached, missed, paused)
- Info pills on hero cards (why-this-zone, why-this-tier, why-this-band)
- Tile labels on progression grids (where there's room)
- Edit chips when the user changes a setting
- Onboarding copy
- Error messages
- "Coming soon" placeholders

## Banned vs. Preferred Patterns

| Banned (consumer-choice voice) | Preferred (coach voice) |
|---|---|
| "Pick Very Active for accurate carb fueling — this preset is built for endurance athletes." | "Carb-fueled plans assume you're burning glycogen daily. Train consistently for a few weeks first, then we'll match the carbs to that work." |
| "High protein at this level is meant for cuts or recomposition — pair with weight training." | "Extra protein at your body fat has nowhere productive to go without a strength stimulus. Add 2–3 lift sessions a week — then protein becomes muscle-building instead of expensive maintenance." |
| "Choose a less aggressive pace." | "A 1% body-weight loss per week is the ceiling your body sustains without losing muscle. Lose Steady gets you there in 2 months without the metabolic backlash." |
| "This option isn't recommended for you." | "Here's what your body actually does when you try this combo: [mechanism]. Switch to [X] and the same effort produces [different outcome]." |
| "Pair this with weight training." | "Muscle is what makes protein useful. Once you're lifting 2–3x/week, this preset starts working for you." |
| "Hard to sustain." | "Most people drop off the wagon at this pace within a month. Lose Steady gets you to the same place in 2 months without the metabolic backlash." |
| "Required field." | "We need your current weight to compute your daily calories. Log it in Bodyweight, then come back." |
| "Goal achieved!" | "You hit your goal weight. Your body's adapted to this calorie level now — most people pick a new pace within a week to keep momentum." |

The pattern: **banned strings prescribe behavior. Preferred strings describe biology and offer a path.**

## Three Voice Modes (Merge All Three)

Every coach-voice surface should hit all three registers simultaneously:

### A. Coach voice (the base)

The 3-pillar rule above. Every sentence runs through this filter.

### B. Progression voice

Every advisory ends with a concrete next step achievable in 1–4 weeks, NOT a vague "consider doing X someday." Even "stay here for now and revisit when your body fat drops below 20%" is a concrete step — the user has a clear marker.

Banned: "you should eventually," "down the line," "in the future you might want to."
Preferred: "right now, here's what works," "in 4 weeks, log your weight again — if you've dropped 4 lb, we'll tighten the deficit."

### C. Path voice

The user is always on a NAMED path with a NEXT named milestone. We don't ask "what do you want to do?" — we say "based on where you are, you're on the [X] path. Next stop: [Y]."

Tier systems across the product (strength's adp zones, cardio's E/T/V zones, carry's BEGINNER → STRONGMAN, Rucking's GoRuck tiers) are all path-voice surfaces. Every domain in the product should graduate to this.

## The Merged Voice Template

When writing any new user-facing string:

> *"You're starting from [acknowledged state]. Here's why your body does [observation]: [biology]. The next checkpoint on your path is [milestone] — most people get there in [timeframe] by [concrete action]."*

Apply this template, then trim for tone. The structure should be invisible to the reader; the rhythm should feel like a coach talking.

## Voice Across Surfaces

The 3-pillar rule is universal, but the rhythm changes per surface:

### In-app surfaces (mobile + web)

Tight, declarative, no marketing flourishes. The user is mid-task; respect their attention.

### Email

Slightly more expansive. Subject lines verb-first ("Confirm your email on MyRX," not "Welcome!"). Bodies in short paragraphs, the action clear. Coach voice still rules.

### SMS / OTP codes

Stripped to facts. "Your MyRX verification code is: 123456." Nothing else.

### Social media

The same coach voice, but accommodating platform conventions. Hashtags allowed where they belong; emojis only when the platform expects them (and even then, sparingly). No "level up," no "game changer," no "transform your life," no "fitness journey."

### Marketing pages

Longer-form, more story-driven. Still coach voice. The reader is evaluating us, not yet using us — but the way we earn their trust is the same way we earn it inside the app: by sounding like someone who knows their body and respects their time.

### Investor / partner communications

Less coach voice, more business-voice — but the underlying clarity, honesty, and mechanism-over-authority principles still hold.

# Section IV — Visual Identity

## Logo System

MyRX has eight locked logo variants, paired across four use cases and two color contexts.

### The Four Logo Types

| Type | Form | Use case |
|---|---|---|
| **Tag** | Wordmark + "Performance Lab" tagline | Hero placements: cover pages, marketing site hero, presentation title slides, signage |
| **Clean** | Wordmark only, single-line treatment | Most contexts: headers, footers, email signatures, business cards |
| **Block** | Wordmark in stacked block treatment | Tight spaces where the wordmark needs to read as a block: square cards, social grid posts |
| **Icon** | Wordmark in square format with safe-zone padding | Favicons, app icons, profile photos, square avatars, system mark |

### The Two Color Contexts

Each type comes in two contrast variants:

- **Black** — for use on light backgrounds. The "My" letters are MyRX Dark (`#121721`).
- **White** — for use on dark backgrounds. The "My" letters are MyRX Foreground (`#F4F3EF`).

The "RX" letters are always **MyRX Lime** (`#CAF240`) regardless of variant. The lime is the brand's signature — it never changes color.

### Choosing the Right Variant

| Surface | Recommended variant |
|---|---|
| App icon (iOS, Android, web favicon) | Icon White (locked) |
| Web app top-left header | Clean White |
| Email signature | Clean White (on dark) or Clean Black (on light) |
| Marketing site hero | Tag White |
| Cover slides (investor decks, brand book) | Tag White |
| Square social media post | Block White or Block Black depending on background |
| Profile photo (LinkedIn, Twitter, Instagram) | Icon White |
| Business card | Clean Black on white card, or Clean White on dark card |
| Print materials | Whichever matches the background |

### Clear Space Rules

The minimum clear space around any logo variant equals **the height of the "M" letter** in that rendering. Nothing else — no text, no other graphics, no UI chrome — may enter that clear space.

For the Icon variant (square), the safe-zone padding is built into the file. The visible wordmark sits inside the inner ~72% of the canvas, leaving ~14% padding on each side. This matches iOS app icon guidelines and Android's adaptive icon safe zone. Do not crop the Icon variant — it's pre-padded.

### Minimum Sizes

Below these sizes, the logo becomes unreadable and should not be used. Use a smaller alternative if needed.

| Variant | Minimum width |
|---|---|
| Tag | 240 px (digital) / 50 mm (print) |
| Clean | 160 px (digital) / 35 mm (print) |
| Block | 80 px (digital) / 20 mm (print) |
| Icon | 32 px (digital) / 10 mm (print) |

### Logo Don'ts

Never:

- **Stretch, skew, or rotate** any logo variant. Always preserve the original aspect ratio.
- **Recolor** the wordmark. The four locked colors are non-negotiable. No gold, no rainbow, no seasonal versions.
- **Add effects** — no drop shadows, no glows, no gradients, no outlines around the letters.
- **Place on busy photographic backgrounds** without a solid-color underlay. If the background is photographic, place the logo inside a dark or light solid panel.
- **Substitute typography.** The wordmark is custom; don't try to recreate it in any other typeface.
- **Crop the Icon variant.** Its padding is intentional and protected by the safe-zone rule.
- **Mix variants** in a single composition. One logo per surface.
- **Render the brand name in plain text** as a substitute for the wordmark. If the wordmark won't fit, redesign the layout — don't fake it with text.

### One Wordmark Per Page Rule

If a page (or screen, or email, or post) has a centered wordmark in the body, the header must NOT also show a wordmark. If the header shows a wordmark, the body must NOT include another one. One mark per surface. Always.

This is the rule that prevents the "branded-into-uselessness" feeling where the logo is repeated three times in one composition. The mark is more powerful when it appears once.

### The Slogan Reservation

The **Tag** variant — wordmark + "Performance Lab" tagline — is reserved for hero placements only. It does not appear in the running app, in email signatures, on social profile avatars, or anywhere else where the wordmark needs to function as a quick identifier.

The tagline is too long to read at small sizes, and overusing it dilutes its impact when it matters. Use the Clean or Icon variant for everyday surfaces; bring out Tag only when the user is about to learn what we do for the first time.

## Color System

### Primary Brand Palette (Locked)

The four colors below are the foundation. Everything else in the visual system supports them.

| Token | Hex | HSL | Role | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **MyRX Lime** | `#CAF240` | `hsl(73°, 87%, 60%)` | Primary accent | Buttons, CTAs, brand highlights, the "RX" letters, any green anywhere in the system |
| **MyRX Dark** | `#121721` | `hsl(220°, 28%, 10%)` | Background | App + web page surface, icon backgrounds, dark email templates |
| **MyRX Surface** | `#171C26` | `hsl(220°, 24%, 12%)` | Elevated surface | Cards, sheets, drawers, modals — the 2%-lighter elevation above Dark |
| **MyRX Foreground** | `#F4F3EF` | `hsl(60°, 5%, 96%)` | Text on dark | Primary text, iconography on dark surfaces. Warm off-white — never pure white. |

#### Why these specific values

- **Lime is yellow-green (H=73)** — closer to yellow than to true green. This is what makes it electric. A true green at H=120 reads as "ecology brand"; our lime reads as "performance."
- **Dark is blue-tinted (H=220)** at moderate saturation (28%) and very low lightness (10%). It looks like black at thumbnail size but reads as "intentional dark" against true `#000000`. The hue sits near-complementary to the lime (H=73 vs H=220 is ~147° apart on the color wheel), so the lime *pops* against this dark instead of blending into the same family. The saturation is what makes it read as blue rather than slate-grey — at lower saturation it would feel cold and corporate; at higher saturation it would feel like a finance app.
- **Surface is the same hue family** as Dark with +2% lightness and slightly lower saturation (24%). This produces the elevation hierarchy users expect (cards sit visibly above the background) without introducing a competing hue.
- **Foreground is warm off-white** (H=60 at very high lightness). Never pure `#FFFFFF`. Pure white is harsh on OLED screens and clashes against the cool dark backgrounds. The warm off-white sits as gentle visual contrast — warm text on cool surface — which is also the canonical pattern for high-contrast readable typography.

## Typography

### The Two Typefaces

**Geist** (sans-serif) — display + body text everywhere. Headlines, paragraphs, button labels, UI copy, marketing pages, this document.

**Geist Mono** (monospaced) — every numeric value in the product. Weights, reps, times, distances, percentages, calorie counts, ages, durations, projections, prices.

Both families are loaded across web and mobile. There are no other typefaces in the system.

### Why These Two

Geist is a modern, neutral sans-serif designed by Vercel — open-source, professionally crafted, and reads cleanly at every size. It's not a "designer" typeface that calls attention to itself; it does its job and gets out of the way. That's the right behavior for a coaching app.

Geist Mono provides tabular alignment for numbers. Numbers in the app change constantly (a user logs a new effort and a projection updates by 5 pounds). With Geist Mono + `tabular-nums`, the digits line up by column — the user sees "135 → 140" as a tight column shift, not a sloppy reflow. This is the kind of precision that makes the product feel built by someone who cared.

### Hierarchy

### Number Rules

- Always pair `Geist Mono` with `font-variant: tabular-nums` for column-aligned digits.
- Big hero numbers (rep-max targets, calorie totals) use `Geist Mono 700` paired with the **TickerNumber** component for slot-machine roll animations on value change. (See Motion Principles below.)

## Motion Principles

Motion is restrained, purposeful, and never decorative. Every animation in the system answers a question: "What is the user's eye doing right now, and what does it need to track?"

### Pattern 1 — Staggered Entrance Cascade

Cards on a detail page slide in sequentially from the bottom (opacity 0→1 + translateY 8→0) with explicit delays of 0 / 250 / 500 ms. The user perceives the page assembling in front of them, which signals "this is data I'm seeing, not a static page."

**Used on:** every detail page top-to-bottom. The 500-ms total entrance is short enough to feel responsive, long enough to register the cascade.

### Pattern 2 — Slot-Machine Numeric Ticker

When a numeric value changes, each digit slot rolls vertically slot-machine style to the new digit. Non-digit characters (×, m, km/h, :, /, %, lb) render as static text inside the same row.

**Used on:** Best-X subtitles in detail page headers, hero card target values, cue-line embedded numbers.
**Not used on:** tiles (status indicators), plate chips (categorical labels), chart axis labels, log-list rows, captions.

The ticker signals progressive achievement. Static numbers signal categorical labels. The distinction is rigid.

### Pattern 3 — Pulsing Chevron

Pairs of chevrons flank a swipeable pill, pulsing in/out to telegraph "you can swipe to navigate." 1.5-second cycle, fade in/out duration 0.25 sec, outer chevron delayed 250 ms behind inner.

**Used on:** BW tier pill row, Weighted Standard adp-zone pill row, Sled Work PUSH/PULL pill, Swimming stroke pill, and any future variant-selector pill row.

### Pattern 4 — Consolidated-Page Swipe

The signature interaction pattern for switching between variants of a consolidated detail page. Whole-page slide with pill physically following the finger, choreographed slide-off + slide-back-in transitions for the pill label, body content scrolling in sync via paged ScrollView.

**Tuned constants** (do not retune by feel):
- Swipe threshold: 20 px
- Slide off-screen: 220 px
- Slide duration: 250 ms
- Pan activate offset: 15 px
- Bounce-back duration: 200 ms

### Pattern 5 — Inline Expansion Panel

Info panels slide in from below using `FadeInUp(200ms)` / `FadeOutUp(180ms)` with sibling `LinearTransition(200ms)` so adjacent content reflows smoothly instead of jumping.

**Used on:** zone info panels, band sub-progression panels, any inline expandable.

### Pattern 6 — PhantomWheel Inertia + Cross-Fade

iOS-style numeric / time / decimal picker with inertia roll. 250 px/s release threshold, deceleration 0.993, halo/center cross-fade by rank.

**Used on:** every value/time/distance input across the app.

### Pattern 7 — Save Button Feedback

After a successful save, the button shows "✓ Saved" in the appropriate domain accent color for 1500 ms before resetting. Disabled during this window to prevent double-tap.

### Universal Rules

- **Cubic-bezier `(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1)`** is the standard easing function for entrances. Snappy at the start, gentle at the end.
- **500 ms** is the standard entrance duration.
- **Never use animations longer than 500 ms** for interactive feedback. Anything longer feels broken.
- **Never animate purely for decoration.** Every animation must communicate something the user needs to track (state change, hierarchy, available interaction).

## Imagery

### Photography Style

Where MyRX uses photography (marketing surfaces, blog content, social media), the style is:

- **Real athletes, not models.** People who actually train, in their actual training environments.
- **Honest lighting.** Natural light, gym light, ambient. No glossy magazine retouching.
- **Mid-action over posed.** The person is in the middle of a lift, a run, a stretch — not posing.
- **Variety of body types, ages, ethnicities.** Fitness is universal; our imagery reflects that.
- **Equipment shown realistically.** A barbell looks like a barbell, not a beauty shot of a barbell.

### What We Avoid

- Stock photos of "fit people in a gym." These read as fake immediately.
- Before/after composites. They commodify body change in a way that contradicts the coach voice.
- Heavy color grading or filter overlays. The brand palette comes from the chrome around the photo, not from the photo itself.
- Sexualized imagery. The product is about performance, not appearance.
- Models holding the product to camera. We don't do "person smiling at phone" stock shots.

### Composition

When photography is used alongside the wordmark or in branded compositions, leave clean negative space for the brand chrome. The photo is the supporting element; the brand voice and call to action are the lead.

# Section VI — Product Vocabulary

The MyRX product uses specific, locked vocabulary. These terms are not interchangeable, and copy across the product must use them consistently. This glossary exists so future contributors don't drift into synonyms.

## Training System

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **adp zone** (adaptation zone) | Which adaptation a strength tile or exercise targets. Three values: **strength** (1–5 reps), **hypertrophy** (6–12 reps), **endurance** (13+ reps). |
| **rep range** | The specific rep count prescribed for a working set. |
| **eff curve** (effort curve) | The rep-max projection formula used by the system. Currently Epley / Brzycki / Lombardi averaged. Translates 1RM → projected weight at any rep count, or logged (weight × reps) → projected 1RM. |
| **max attempt** | A milestone rep count on bodyweight movements. We do not use "max reps" for bodyweight; we use "max attempts." |
| **tier** | A proficiency level within a progression system. Strength tiers (BEGINNER, INTERMEDIATE, ADVANCED, STRONGMAN for carry). BW assist tiers (Full RX, Band, Knee, Band+Knee). Cardio zones (ENDURANCE, THRESHOLD, VO2 MAX). |
| **next step** | The single concrete action a user can take this week to progress on their current path. Every coaching surface in the product surfaces exactly one. |

## Coaching Relationships

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **coach** | A subscriber to the B2B2C arm — runs a roster of clients on MyRX. Lowercase in copy unless starting a sentence. |
| **athlete / client** | The end user. "Athlete" preferred in product copy; "client" only when emphasizing the coach-client relationship. |
| **admin** | A MyRX platform administrator (the operating team). Distinct from "coach." |
| **self-coached** | A user who is not paired with a coach — they use MyRX directly via App Store / Play Store / Stripe Web. |
| **invite** | The act of a coach extending an offer to an athlete to join MyRX on their roster. |
| **accept** | The act of an athlete accepting a coach invite. |
| **swap** | When an athlete who is already on one coach's roster accepts an invite from a different coach. The new relationship replaces the old. |

# Section VII — Brand Guardrails

What we never do, in any context, without an explicit override:

### Voice guardrails

- **Never use consumer-choice voice.** "Pick X for Y" is banned. Use coach voice (acknowledge → biology → next step).
- **Never use authority instead of mechanism.** "Experts recommend" and "research shows" are not allowed in user-facing copy.
- **Never use vague future hedges.** "Consider," "you might want to," "eventually," "down the line" are banned.
- **Never use marketing clichés.** "Transform your life," "fitness journey," "level up," "game changer," "unlock," "discover the secret," "before/after that will shock you" — all banned.
- **Never gamify reality.** No streaks that punish missed days. No badges for compliance. No leaderboards that compare athletes against each other.
- **Never speak about the user's body in shame language.** "Your problem areas," "trouble spots" — banned. Bodies are biological, not deficient.

### Visual guardrails

- **Never use pure black (`#000000`) on dark surfaces.** Always MyRX Dark (`#121721`).
- **Never use pure white (`#FFFFFF`) on dark surfaces.** Always MyRX Foreground (`#F4F3EF`).
- **Never substitute brand colors with off-brand equivalents.** No alternate lime shades, no different greens.
- **Never use the lime as decorative chrome.** Lime is for primary CTAs and brand highlights only.
- **Never mix typefaces.** Geist and Geist Mono only.
- **Never use emojis as icon substitutes.** Lucide icons only.
- **Never stretch, recolor, or modify the logo.** The eight locked variants are the only acceptable forms.
- **Never crop the Icon variant.** Its safe-zone padding is intentional.

### Product guardrails

- **Never auto-create accounts on behalf of a user.** Always require explicit signup.
- **Never auto-enter financial information.** Bank, credit card, account numbers must be entered by the user.
- **Never delete a user's data without explicit confirmation.** Deletions require multi-step opt-in.
- **Never share user data with third parties without explicit consent.** Privacy is a brand value, not a legal afterthought.
- **Never claim coaching efficacy we can't substantiate.** If a feature is in beta, say so. If a projection is an estimate, label it.
- **Never use dark patterns.** No nag screens, no manipulative upsells, no hidden cancel buttons.

### Cross-platform guardrails

- **Never let web and mobile drift.** When a decision lands, it lands on both surfaces simultaneously.
- **Never let admin surfaces diverge from end-user surfaces in look and feel.** The admin portal is denser, but it uses the same colors, the same typography, the same components.
- **Never ship a brand-mark variant not listed in the logo system.** If a new variant is needed, add it to the brand book first, then ship it.

If you see MyRX branding being used incorrectly — by a competitor, a partner who has gone off-brand, a coach building their own marketing — report it to **team@myrxfit.com**. We take brand integrity seriously and will work with the parties involved to bring usage into compliance.

## Brand Updates

This document is versioned. Major updates (new audience, new domain, new visual identity) are released as a new version number (1.0 → 2.0). Minor updates (clarifications, additional examples, typo fixes) bump the patch (1.0 → 1.1).

When the brand book changes:

1. The new version is committed to the repository
2. CLAUDE.md is updated to point at the new version's path
3. All active surfaces are audited for compliance with the changes
4. An announcement goes out to coaches and partners with a summary

The brand book is a contract with the future. We don't update it lightly.

## Voice and Tone Authority

The voice and tone rules in **Section III** are owned by the MyRX team and are non-delegable. They cannot be overridden by individual contributors, marketing partners, or external agencies without explicit team sign-off.

If a piece of copy needs to break a voice rule for a legitimate reason (legal disclaimer language, regulated medical content, etc.), the team reviews and approves the exception, and the rule itself may be updated if the exception reveals a gap.

## Visual System Authority

The visual system in **Section IV** is owned by the MyRX team and the color palette + typography are locked at the code level (in `web/src/index.css` and `mobile/src/theme.ts`). Changes to those tokens require:

1. A design review against this brand book
2. A migration plan for existing surfaces
3. Cross-platform synchronization (web + mobile simultaneously)
4. CLAUDE.md update

Individual contributors cannot introduce new colors, typefaces, or icon libraries without going through this process.
